Part 1 — Strategic Analysis

Why Data Centers Cluster in South India While Solar IPPs Choose Rajasthan & Gujarat

The Core Logic (One Paragraph)

Data centers and solar farms have fundamentally opposite locational requirements. Data centers are demand-centric infrastructure — they cluster near latency-sensitive consumers, submarine cable landing stations, reliable grids, and coastal water access. Solar IPPs are supply-centric infrastructure — they chase maximum irradiation, vast barren land, and favorable open-access economics. This structural divergence explains why India's digital infrastructure map looks nothing like its renewable energy map.

The Structural Divergence

🖥
Data Centers follow:
Submarine cables → IT demand clusters → Coastal water → Stable grid → Skilled workforce
☀️
Solar IPPs follow:
Maximum GHI irradiation → Vast arid land → Low land cost → ISTS evacuation corridors → Nearby industrial demand

Why Tamil Nadu & Andhra Pradesh Win (for Data Centers)

1

Submarine Cable Access — Irreplaceable Moat

Tamil Nadu houses over 30% of India's subsea cables with 6 cables totaling 14.8 TBPS capacity. SEA-ME-WE-6 landed at Chennai in Feb 2025 adding 220 TBPS. Andhra Pradesh is building its first cable landing station at Vizag (Sify's open CLS). No amount of cheap inland power can substitute this — latency is non-negotiable for AI workloads and global cloud.

Source: TN DC Policy 2021 (it.tn.gov.in); W.Media Chennai analysis

2

Aggressive State Policy & Incentive Design

TN DC Policy 2021: 100% electricity duty waiver for 5 years, 100% stamp duty waiver in Tier C districts, 50% in A/B, concessional open access charges, dual power grid access, and single-window approvals within 90 days. AP DC Policy 4.0 (2024–29): 6 GW target, 25% land cost discount, 100% stamp duty exemption, 10% capex subsidy on machinery for 10 years.

Source: it.tn.gov.in; teamleaseregtech.com (AP Policy 4.0)

3

Water Availability & Coastal Cooling

A 100 MW data center consumes ~2 million litres/day for cooling (IEA 2025). Coastal Tamil Nadu and AP have access to seawater cooling — AP specifically promotes seawater cooling for coastal DCs (CEEW 2026). Rajasthan has among India's lowest per-capita water availability (NITI Aayog); Gujarat's Kutch region is severely water-stressed. This constraint alone rules out Rajasthan/Gujarat for traditional evaporative cooling systems.

Source: CEEW Feb 2026 (ceew.in); IEA 2025; Takshashila Oct 2025

4

Seismic Risk Profile

Hyderabad and Visakhapatnam are in Seismic Zone II (lowest risk category, BIS IS 1893). Most of Tamil Nadu and AP's Deccan plateau region is in Zone II. In contrast, Gujarat's Kutch region is in Seismic Zone V — India's highest risk zone (Bhuj 2001 earthquake: 7.7 magnitude, 20,000 deaths). Mission-critical Tier III/IV data centers explicitly avoid Zone IV/V locations for insurance and resilience reasons.

Source: PIB Seismic Zones (pib.gov.in); BIS IS 1893:2002

5

Electricity Tariffs & Open Access Economics

Tamil Nadu: ~₹5.8/unit average (national low); 100% CSS exemption for solar OA under green energy policy. AP: ~₹6.8/unit with 100% CSS + Additional Surcharge waiver for solar OA (5 years). For comparison, Rajasthan: ~₹7.9/unit (comparatively high due to transmission costs over large state). Gujarat: ~₹5.2/unit but additional surcharge increased to ₹1/kWh Oct 2025. Landed solar OA cost: Maharashtra highest (₹8+/kWh), Telangana and UP lowest (₹5–₹6/kWh).

Source: Mercom India Q3 2025; Forum of Regulators Open Access Report

6

Skilled Workforce — Often Underrated

Tamil Nadu provides the largest technically qualified IT workforce nationally — 7 lakh+ direct IT workers; 18 IT/ITeS SEZs; SaaS capital of India (W.Media). Hyderabad has Google, Microsoft, Meta campuses creating deep data center operations talent. Rajasthan (Jaipur) and Gujarat (Ahmedabad/Surat) lack pools of data center operations engineers, power systems specialists, and AI infrastructure professionals needed for hyperscale facilities.

Source: W.Media Chennai ecosystem report; storyboard18.com Dec 2025

Why Rajasthan & Gujarat Win (for Solar IPPs like Excell Solar)

☀️ Solar Irradiation — The Primary Driver

Rajasthan receives 5.5–6.0 kWh/m²/day GHI across the Thar Desert; Gujarat averages 5.2–5.8 kWh/m²/day. Tamil Nadu and AP coastal regions average 4.5–5.2 kWh/m²/day — 10–25% lower annually. For a solar IPP, this directly determines competitive PPA tariff: ~₹2.5–₹2.8/unit in RJ/GJ vs ₹2.9–₹3.2/unit in coastal states. Rajasthan had the highest installed renewable capacity nationally at 29,981 MW; Gujarat second at 29,525 MW (IEEFA Oct 2024).

Source: IEEFA Oct 2024 (ieefa.org); Mercom India Solar Open Access 2025

🏜 Vast Non-Agricultural Land at Low Cost

Utility-scale solar requires 4–5 acres/MW. Rajasthan's Thar Desert has millions of acres of barren, non-agricultural land at ₹10–₹50 lakh/acre vs ₹3–₹15 crore/acre in coastal DC zones. Bhadla Solar Park spans 14,000+ acres. Gujarat's Rann of Kutch similarly offers large flat tracts at minimal cost. Data centers need only 3–50 acres but require proximity to cable stations, fiber, and labor — none available in desert regions.

Source: Avaada.com land analysis; IEEFA briefing note 2024

⚡ Open Access & CSS Waiver Economics

Rajasthan has India's largest open access solar pipeline at ~10.2 GW (Mercom, June 2025) and ~2.4 GW installed solar OA capacity (9.8% of national total). Rajasthan offers full CSS waiver for solar OA; Gujarat gives 100% CSS waiver for solar. Rajasthan's battery storage policy is "most aggressive for open access" (Mercom Oct 2025) — requiring ≥5 MW solar projects to include storage. Landed cost in RJ/GJ: ₹5–₹6/kWh vs DISCOM ₹7.5–₹9/kWh — 20–35% savings for nearby industrial consumers.

Source: Mercom India Rajasthan OA analysis Oct 2025; ICRA RE report 2023

🏭 Nearby Industrial Demand (Cement, Chemicals, Mining)

Solar IPPs like Excell Solar serve energy-intensive industries in their neighboring states — cement plants, mining operations, chemical complexes, textile mills — that are heavy electricity consumers with industrial tariffs of ₹7–₹9/unit. This makes solar OA commercially attractive. Data centers, by contrast, can sign PPAs with solar developers regardless of geographic proximity — they don't need to be co-located with the solar plant. A Chennai DC can have its renewable PPA sourced from Rajasthan via ISTS.

Source: JMK Research open access viability; Forum of Regulators (FOR) OA report

Consultant's Final Verdict

The Real Bet

This is not a data center investment. This is not an energy procurement decision. This is a structural repositioning of Google as a digital + energy infrastructure company — internalizing energy as a core production input for AI, not a utility cost.

India's geography explains the strategy: coastal states (Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh) win the hyperscale data center race because they offer the irreplaceable combination of submarine cable latency, stable coastal water access, low seismic risk, and aggressive policy support. Rajasthan and Gujarat dominate solar IPP deployments because they offer the irreplaceable combination of peak irradiation, vast cheap land, and the ISTS evacuation corridors that solar economics demand.

Google is front-running a future where hyperscalers become parallel utilities. The asymmetric bet: if India's power inefficiencies persist + AI demand explodes + regulatory flexibility continues → massive structural advantage. If even one breaks → capital locked in low-return, regulated assets.

The Core Strategic Insight

Data centers follow data and connectivity, not cheap power. Solar IPPs follow irradiation and land, not demand. India's energy map and its digital infrastructure map are structurally divergent — and that divergence is not a problem to solve but a feature to arbitrage.

What Most Analysts Miss

The real competition in the AI era is not just in compute or cloud — it is in who controls the cheapest, cleanest, and most reliable electrons at scale. Google's distribution license is not regulatory arbitrage. It is the first move in building a private energy operating system for AI infrastructure.
